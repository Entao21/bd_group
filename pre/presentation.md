Greenland's ice sheet is losing mass rapidly, with an average loss of about 150 ± 13 Gt per year between 1992 and 2020 ([IMBIE Team, 2020](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-019-1855-2)). That huge amout of ice loss means on average, Greenland ice loss alone contributed 0.42 mm to sea-level rise per year, accounting for about 14.7% of global sea-level rise ([IMBIE Team, 2020](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-019-1855-2); [Otosaka et al., 2023](https://essd.copernicus.org/articles/15/1597/2023/); [van den Broeke et al., 2016](https://tc.copernicus.org/articles/10/1933/2016/)). Climate change, global warming, human activities,  mineral exploration, and many other factors are all threatening this sensitive cryospheric environment ([Meredith et al.,2024](https://www.ipcc.ch/srocc/chapter/chapter-3-2/)). 

Among all parts of Greenland's ice sheet, peripheral glaciers are the most vulnerable and most overlooked. According to [Larocca et al., 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-023-01855-6), peripheral glaciers are not only melting at a faster rate than the central ice sheet, but their melting speed is also accelerating more rapidly than the central ice sheet. Research by [Khan et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1029/2022GL098915) on North Greenland peripheral glaciers further supports this conclusion. Although the area of interest in their research accounts for only 4% of the total area of Greenland's ice sheet, they found that this small area contributed about 14% of current Greenland ice loss ([Khan et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1029/2022GL098915)).

Currently, several glacier monitoring products and platforms exist, but each addresses only part of the picture. For example, the Randolph Glacier Inventory provides static glacier outlines for a single reference epoch rather than continuous change detection([RGI Consortium, 2023](https://nsidc.org/data/nsidc-0770/versions/7)). The IMBIE mass balance assessments (IMBIE Team, 2020) report aggregated ice-sheet-wide mass loss without spatially explicit retreat mapping at the individual glacier scale; and the PROMICE network (Fausto et al., 2021) delivers point-based weather station observations that cannot be directly linked to spatial patterns of glacier change. Meanwhile, mining licence registries and mineral exploration datasets are maintained in entirely separate administrative systems, making it difficult for researchers, planners, or policymakers to visually compare climate-driven cryospheric change with the footprint of human activity. No existing tool integrates multi-temporal glacier extent, climate warming trends, and anthropogenic proximity indicators into a single interactive interface — a gap this application aims to fill.
